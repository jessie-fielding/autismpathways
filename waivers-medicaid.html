Your Rights

You have the right to appeal any Medicaid denial.

💜

This is the part most families don't know about until it's too late. You have a window to appeal — and many appeals are won when families show up with the right documentation. Please don't wait on this one.

⏰ Deadlines vary by state Your denial notice should include the specific deadline and instructions for how to file. Read it carefully and act before the deadline passes. If you're unsure, call the agency and ask: "What is the deadline to request a fair hearing?"

When appealing makes sense:

  • ✓ You believe the denial was based on incorrect information
  • ✓ You have documentation that wasn't included in the original application
  • ✓ The denial reason doesn't match your situation as you understand it
  • ✓ You were denied under a category that may not have been the right one for your child

Getting Started

Applying is always the right first step.

👋

Even if you are not sure you will qualify, applying tells you which path to take next. Both an approval and a denial have a clear forward path — and you cannot take either one without applying first.

💡 Why this matters for your child Medicaid for your child is separate from household Medicaid. Even if your family income is too high for regular Medicaid, your child may qualify through a disability-based pathway. Always ask specifically about this when you apply.
📋 What this guide covers We will walk you through exactly what to gather, where to apply, what to say, and what to do after — so you do not have to take notes or figure it out alone.
1
Before You Apply

Gather these before you start.

Having everything ready before you open the application will save you from stopping and restarting. Most applications time out after 30–60 minutes.

FOR YOUR CHILD

  • ✓
    Child birth certificate
  • ✓
    Child Social Security card or number
  • ✓
    Any existing diagnosis reports (autism, developmental delay, etc.)
  • ✓
    Child current insurance card (if any)

FOR YOUR HOUSEHOLD

  • ✓
    Recent pay stubs (last 30 days) for all working adults
  • ✓
    Most recent federal tax return
  • ✓
    Proof of address (utility bill or lease)
  • ✓
    Social Security numbers for all household members
⚡ Pro tipTake photos of all documents before you go. Even if you apply online, you may need to upload or fax them later.
2
Where to Apply

Online, in person, or by phone?

Each method has real trade-offs. Here is what to know before you choose.

💻 Online — recommended for most
Apply through your state Medicaid portal or benefits.gov. Save and return anytime, upload documents directly, and get a confirmation number instantly.
✓ Best if you have all your documents ready
🏢 In person — best for disability pathway
Visit your local Medicaid or DHHS office. Often the best option if applying for a disability-based pathway — you can ask questions directly and make sure your child situation is documented correctly.
✓ Best if your child has a diagnosis or developmental delay
📞 By phone
Call your state Medicaid office to apply over the phone. Good if you have trouble with websites, but harder to track what was submitted.
⚠ Always ask for a reference number and write it down
📍 Find your state officeGo to medicaid.gov/state-overviews and select your state. Or visit the Contacts section of this app for your state direct number.
3
What to Say

The exact words that matter.

These phrases flag your child case for the right pathway. Without them, your application may be processed as a standard household income case and denied.

📞 Script 1 — Opening the application

"I am applying for Medicaid for my child who has a developmental disability. I want to make sure this is processed as a disability-based application, not just a household income application. Can you tell me what pathway is available for children with autism in your state?"

📞 Script 2 — If they say you do not qualify

"I understand household income may be above the threshold, but I have read that children with disabilities may qualify through a separate pathway. Can you check specifically for a disability-based Medicaid category for my child?"

📞 Script 3 — Before you hang up or submit

"Can I get a reference number or confirmation for this application? And when should I expect a decision and how will it be communicated?"

📝 Write down every contactDate, time, name of person you spoke with, reference number, and what they told you. This protects you if something goes wrong later.
4
After You Apply

What happens next — and when to follow up.

1

You receive a confirmation

Should arrive within 1–3 days by mail or email. Save this — it has your application number.

2

Processing period (30–45 days)

Most states must decide within 45 days. Disability-based applications can take up to 90 days in some states.

3

Follow up at 30 days

If you have not heard anything, call and ask for a status update. Reference your application number.

4

Decision arrives by mail

Approval or denial — both have a clear next step. Save the letter either way.

❌ If deniedThis is not the end. Return here and select "We were denied" — the full denial pathway walks you through your rights, appeal options, and the disability determination pathway.

Autism Pathways — Medicaid Action Guide

Medicaid denial guidance and next steps

Important: This guide is for educational and organizational purposes only. It does not constitute legal, medical, or benefits advice. Rules and programs vary by state and change frequently. Always confirm current details directly with your state's agency before submitting any application.

1. Your Immediate Action Steps

  1. Save the denial letter — note the date it was issued and the exact reason listed. You'll need both.
  2. Call the agency and ask them to explain the reason in plain language — it's okay to say you don't understand the letter.
  3. Ask: "Is there a disability-based Medicaid pathway for my child?" — many caseworkers won't volunteer this. You have to ask directly.
  4. Ask about your right to appeal — deadlines vary by state and are listed on the denial notice.
  5. Consider starting a disability determination process in parallel — you don't have to wait for the appeal to resolve before starting this.

2. When to Appeal

  • ☐ You believe the denial was based on incorrect information
  • ☐ You have documentation that wasn't included in the original application
  • ☐ The denial reason doesn't match your situation as you understand it
  • ☐ You were denied under a category that may not have been the right one

3. Document Gathering Checklist

  • ☐ Denial letter — note the date issued and the exact reason listed
  • ☐ All diagnostic evaluations (autism, developmental, psychological)
  • ☐ Medical records from pediatrician and specialists
  • ☐ Therapy notes and progress reports (OT, PT, SLP, ABA)
  • ☐ School IEP or eligibility documentation
  • ☐ Written description of your child's daily support needs
  • ☐ Documentation of safety concerns (elopement, self-injury, supervision needs)
  • ☐ Any previous Medicaid correspondence or reference numbers

4. What to Say When You Call

Asking about disability pathway

"I received a denial for household Medicaid. My child has [diagnosis] and significant support needs. Is there a separate disability-based Medicaid pathway for my child? Who should I speak with about that?"

Asking about appeal

"I received a denial notice and I want to understand my right to appeal. What is the deadline to request a fair hearing, and what is the process?"

Asking about disability determination

"I want to start the disability determination process for my child. Which office handles that in this state, and what documentation do I need to bring?"

5. What to Do While You Wait

  • ☐ Continue gathering documentation — more is always better
  • ☐ Keep a log of every call: date, name of person, what was said, any reference numbers
  • ☐ Ask your child's pediatrician to document support needs in the medical chart
  • ☐ Contact your school district about IEP services — these are separate from Medicaid
  • ☐ Look into your state's disability services office (separate from Medicaid)
  • ☐ Connect with a parent advocacy organization in your state for guidance

This guide is for educational and organizational purposes only. Always confirm current details with your local Medicaid agency. | autismpathways.app
